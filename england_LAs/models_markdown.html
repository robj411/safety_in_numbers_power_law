Road traffic injuries: data and regression models

Data

First lets look at the basic log-log relationship between number of cyclist injuries and distance travelled (over 11 years and across 148 areas).

These plots are descriptive: were expecting to see a positive correlation, i.e.the more cycling there is, the more injuries to cyclists there are.

And now lets look at the difference between all the areas and the urban areas (in pink, defined as the areas with >99% of the population living in an urban area (city, town or minor conurbation) in the 2011 census).

So is it possible that we get the safety-in-numbers results as a result of having all high-density (urban) areas having lower levels of cycling? I.e., all the high-cycling places are rural and sparse? Yes: there is a relationship between total road length and total cycling, with urban areas having the shortest total road length (excepting the Isles of Scilly) and not the most total cycling.

Now lets look at the rates (the numbers per distance travelled).

These plots should be more diagnostic: we should expect to see a negative correlation if the more cycling there is, the fewer injuries there are per cyclist.

Note that there are fewer events for serious injuries and fewer still fatalities. This results in the straight diagonal lines, and indicate that the definition of rate is not useful here.
